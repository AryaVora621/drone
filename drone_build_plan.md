🚀 Master Blueprint & Project Goal: A2212 Custom ESP32 Drone🎯 Project Goal (For AI Agents)Objective: Build a low-cost, fully functional 5-inch quadcopter (capable of carrying a GoPro) using standard, easily accessible electronics, and a modular 3D-printed frame.Core Architecture:Flight Controller: ESP32 DevKit V1 paired with an MPU6050 (I2C) running an open-source flight stack (like ESP-FC / Betaflight).Remote Controller: A "Relay Station" ESP32 that receives Bluetooth commands from a PS4 (DualShock 4) controller and instantly relays them to the drone via ESP-NOW for ultra-low latency and extended range (100m+).Power System: 3S 18650 Li-ion Battery pack (12.6V).Propulsion: Four A2212 2200KV Brushless Motors driven by four SimonK 30A ESCs spinning 5045 (5-inch) propellers.📊 1. The Physics & Reality CheckBefore we build, you need to understand the physics of the hardware you just bought:The Weight: Your motors (50g x 4 = 200g), battery (~150g), ESCs (~100g), and frame/electronics (~100g) mean this drone will weigh roughly 550 grams.The Thrust: On a 3S battery with 5045 (5-inch) propellers, a single 2200KV motor generates roughly 700g of thrust. Total thrust = ~2,800 grams.The Result: You have a Thrust-to-Weight ratio of about 5:1. This is fantastic! It will easily lift a GoPro (~120g) and still have plenty of punch for flips and rolls.Warning on the Battery: A 3S 18650 pack can typically output 20 to 30 Amps continuously. If you punch the throttle to 100%, all four motors will demand 60+ Amps and your battery will "sag" (drop voltage quickly). Keep your aggressive flying to short bursts.🖨️ 2. 3D Printing Strategy (Bambu Lab A1 Mini)Your Bambu A1 Mini is an incredible, fast printer, but its 180x180x180mm build volume presents a challenge. A 5-inch drone needs a motor-to-motor diagonal of about 210mm to 250mm. A single-piece frame will not fit.The Solution: The "Peon230" Modular FrameThe Model: Search Thingiverse for "Peon230" (Thing: 629338). It is a legendary, heavily tested split-arm design. The base plate and arms print separately and bolt together with M3 screws, fitting perfectly on the A1 Mini!Material: Print this in PETG or ABS/ASA. Do NOT use PLA. The A2212 motors get very hot and will literally melt PLA arms mid-flight.Print Orientation: Print the arms lying completely flat. You want the layer lines running lengthwise down the arm for maximum strength. Use 5 wall loops and 40% Gyroid infill.🔌 3. Hardware & Wiring ArchitectureBecause you are using 4 separate 30A ESCs, power distribution is critical.Power DistributionYou need to split the 12.6V from your 3S battery into 4 thick wire pairs to feed the ESCs.DIY Method: Strip the ends of 4 red ESC wires, twist them together with the battery's red wire, and solder them into a massive joint. Wrap heavily in electrical tape. Repeat for the black (ground) wires.The Flight Controller (ESP32)[3S Battery 12.6V] 
       |
       +--> [30A ESC] --> A2212 Motor 1 (Front Left - CW)
       +--> [30A ESC] --> A2212 Motor 2 (Front Right - CCW)
       +--> [30A ESC] --> A2212 Motor 3 (Back Right - CW)
       +--> [30A ESC] --> A2212 Motor 4 (Back Left - CCW)
       |
       +--> (Wait! How do we power the ESP32?)
CRITICAL STEP: Most 30A SimonK ESCs have a built-in BEC (Battery Eliminator Circuit). Look at the 3 thin wires coming out of the ESC.The Red wire provides a safe 5V.Connect the Red wire from only ONE ESC to the VIN or 5V pin of your ESP32. (Clip the red wires on the other 3 ESCs so they don't fight each other).Sensor Mounting (The Most Important Step)The A2212 motors create heavy vibrations.You must soft-mount the MPU6050. Use thick double-sided foam tape.Connect the MPU to the ESP32 using highly flexible, thin silicone wire (keep it under 5cm) to prevent stiff wires from transmitting vibrations directly into the sensor chip.🎮 4. Software: The Relay ArchitectureYou are building a distributed system using two ESP32s communicating via ESP-NOW.The Desk Relay (Transmitter)Connects via Bluetooth to the PS4 Controller using the PS4Controller library.Maps joystick values (-127 to +127) to standard RC channels (1000 to 2000).Blasts this data over ESP-NOW to the drone's specific MAC address every 10 milliseconds.The Drone (Receiver / Flight Controller)Receives ESP-NOW packets.Reads MPU6050 Gyro over I2C.Runs a 250Hz+ PID loop.Outputs 50Hz PWM / OneShot signals to the 4 ESCs.
